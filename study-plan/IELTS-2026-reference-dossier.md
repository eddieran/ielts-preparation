# IELTS Academic 2026 — Research Dossier

This dossier is built for a learner with roughly one month to prepare, whose **weak areas are Writing and Speaking** and who needs **warm-up (not rebuild) for Listening and Reading**. It folds in adversarial verification: outdated book editions are corrected, unverifiable "statistics" are flagged or dropped, and live-official resources are prioritised over third-party ones. The single biggest 2026 development is that IELTS is going **computer-only from mid-2026** (paper discontinued in most markets around 27 June 2026), so practise on screen. Content, question types, the 0–9 band scale and the May-2023 band descriptors are **unchanged** — only the delivery method is moving. Spend the bulk of your month on Writing Task 2 and Speaking Part 3 (where bands are decided), and treat Listening/Reading as daily timed warm-ups.

> Sourced and verified by a 15-agent research workflow on 2026-06-28. Overall confidence: **HIGH** (format, scoring, band-descriptor wording, delivery changes and One Skill Retake rules all corroborated by live official sources). See "Verification notes" at the bottom for exactly what was corrected.

## Test format & recent changes

Total test time ~2 hours 45 minutes. Listening → Reading → Writing run back-to-back with **no breaks**; Speaking is the same day or up to 7 days before/after.

| Section | Time | Questions / Tasks | Structure |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Listening** | ~30 min (+10 min transfer on paper only; ~2 min review on computer) | 40 (4 parts × 10) | 4 recordings, heard **once**. Part 1 everyday conversation; Part 2 social monologue (often map/plan); Part 3 academic discussion (up to 4 speakers); Part 4 academic mini-lecture |
| **Reading (Academic)** | 60 min (**includes** time to record answers — no separate/extra transfer time) | 40 | 3 passages, 2,150–2,750 words total, increasing difficulty |
| **Writing (Academic)** | 60 min | 2 tasks | Task 1 ≥150 words (~20 min, describe a visual); Task 2 ≥250 words (~40 min, essay). **Task 2 is double-weighted** |
| **Speaking** | 11–14 min | 3 parts | Part 1 interview (4–5 min); Part 2 long turn (1 min prep, speak 1–2 min); Part 3 discussion (4–5 min) |

**Paper vs computer.** Same content, timing and scoring. The decisive practical difference is in Listening: paper gives a 10-minute transfer window; computer makes you type as you go with only ~2 minutes to review. Reading has no extra transfer time in either mode. Listening & Reading are auto-marked; Writing & Speaking are always marked by trained human examiners (Speaking is always with a real examiner, in person or live video call — never AI-marked).

**2026 delivery change (verified).** From mid-2026 IELTS is no longer offered on paper — "all IELTS tests will be delivered on computer" (timelines vary by market). A new **"Writing on Paper"** hybrid exists in selected markets (Listening/Reading on computer, Writing handwritten). UKVI SELT is fully digital only. Confirm your local centre's status when booking.

**One Skill Retake (OSR).** Retake just **one** of the four skills, keeping the other three scores. Computer-delivered only, must be booked within **60 days** of the original test, **once per full test**, same delivery mode. **Not available in the USA.** This is strategically useful: diagnose your weakest skill early and keep OSR in reserve.

**Results & validity.** Computer results in ~1–5 days (often 1–2); paper results at 13 days. TRF is recommended valid for **2 years** (based on language-attrition research); older results accepted at receiving organisations' discretion.

**Integrity (verified vs reported).** Since 22 Feb 2025, paper centres require a black ballpoint pen (provided). Examiners are trained to detect and disregard memorised/template language — *confirmed*. The specific "Writing capped at ~Band 4" figure for memorised content is **reported coaching guidance, not an official published number**; treat it as directional.

## How it is scored

Each skill is reported 0–9 in whole/half bands. **Overall = average of the four skill bands, rounded to the nearest half band** (an average ending in .25 rounds up to the next half; .75 rounds up to the next whole). Worked example: L7 + R6.5 + W6 + S6.5 = 6.5 average → 6.5.

- **Listening / Reading**: 40 raw marks each, converted to a band. No negative marking. Indicative Academic Reading conversion (commonly cited, not officially fixed): 39–40 = 9; 37–38 = 8.5; 35–36 = 8; 33–34 = 7.5; 30–32 = 7; 27–29 = 6.5; 23–26 = 6; 19–22 = 5.5; 15–18 = 5. Academic Reading is graded harder than General Training at the same raw score.
- **Writing**: Task 1 and Task 2 each scored on four equal criteria; the two task scores combine with Task 2 weighted double; the overall Writing band is rounded to the nearest half. One weak criterion drags the whole band (TR7+CC7+LR6+GRA6 = 6.5).
- **Speaking**: four equal criteria (25% each), averaged.

### Writing — Band 6 vs 7 vs 8 (weak area; descriptors verified verbatim, May 2023)

| Criterion | Band 6 | Band 7 | Band 8 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Task Response (T2) / Achievement (T1)** | Addresses all parts but unevenly; position may become unclear/repetitive; ideas relevant but under-developed | Addresses all parts; **clear position throughout**; ideas extended & supported (may over-generalise) | Sufficiently addresses all parts; well-developed, relevant, extended & supported response |
| **Coherence & Cohesion** | Coherent overall but cohesion faulty/mechanical; referencing not always clear | Logical progression throughout; range of cohesive devices (some over/under-use); **one clear central topic per paragraph** | Sequences logically; manages all cohesion well; appropriate paragraphing |
| **Lexical Resource** | Adequate range; attempts less-common vocab with inaccuracy; errors don't impede | Sufficient range for flexibility/precision; less-common items with **collocation awareness**; occasional errors | Wide range, fluent & flexible, precise meanings; skilful uncommon items |
| **Grammatical Range & Accuracy** | Mix of simple/complex; some errors that rarely impede | Variety of complex structures; **frequent error-free sentences**; good control | Wide range; **majority of sentences error-free**; only occasional errors |

The decisive 6→7 moves: a position held *throughout*, each idea actually developed (explanation + example), one topic per paragraph, natural (not mechanical) linking, accurate collocation, and *frequent* error-free complex sentences.

### Speaking — Band 6 vs 7 vs 8 (weak area)

| Criterion | Band 6 | Band 7 | Band 8 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fluency & Coherence** | Speaks at length but coherence breaks with repetition/self-correction/hesitation; over-uses connectives | Speaks at length without noticeable effort; only language-related hesitation; range of connectives flexibly | Fluent, only occasional repetition; hesitation is **content-related (thinking what to say)**, not language-related |
| **Lexical Resource** | Enough vocab to discuss at length despite inappropriacies; generally paraphrases | Some less-common & idiomatic vocab; style/collocation awareness; paraphrases effectively | Wide resource used readily & flexibly; skilful idiomatic/uncommon items |
| **Grammatical Range & Accuracy** | Mix of short/complex with limited flexibility; complex forms usually contain errors | Range of complex structures with some flexibility; **frequent error-free sentences** | Wide range used flexibly; most sentences error-free |
| **Pronunciation** | Range of features, mixed control; generally understood | All Band 6 features **plus some Band 8**; range with flexibility; L1 accent has minimal effect | Wide range, sustained flexible use; easy to understand throughout |

Key rule (verified): for Band 7 in Pronunciation **and** Fluency you must show *all* Band 6 features **plus some** Band 8 — i.e., pauses are about ideas, not searching for words. (Correction: the full 9-point Pronunciation scale is **not** a 2025 change — it has defined odd bands since August 2008.)

## Writing Task 1 (Academic)

**What it is.** Describe/summarise a visual in ≥150 words in ~20 min; worth one-third of Writing. Most coaches do Task 2 first because it is double-weighted. Ideal length 150–190; going past ~200 wastes time without raising the score. It is purely descriptive — **no opinion, no conclusion**.

**Question types (all must be practised — unpredictable).** Line graph, bar chart, pie chart, table, process/cycle diagram, map (usually two, showing change over time), and **mixed/multiple charts**. Recent trackers report "dynamic" charts (line/bar over time) dominating and environmental/energy themes in a large share of papers (third-party observation, not official).

**Fixed 4-paragraph structure.**
1. **Introduction** — one sentence paraphrasing the prompt (change *shows → illustrates/depicts/compares*).
2. **Overview** — 2–3 big-picture features (highest/lowest, biggest change, start vs end, number of stages) signalled with "Overall,". **No specific numbers.** This is the gate to Band 7.
3. **Body 1** — a logically grouped set of data with selective figures + comparison.
4. **Body 2** — the remaining data with figures + comparison.

**Criteria focus.** Task Achievement turns on a clear overview + selecting and grouping the most significant features (not listing every point). A response with genuinely **no overview attempt typically falls to Band 4–5** — a widely-taught heuristic, not an exact official sentence (official Band 5 wording is "presents an overview with information inappropriately selected").

**Useful language.**
- Trend verbs (use verb + noun forms): rise/increase/grow/climb/surge; fall/decline/drop/plummet; fluctuate; level off/plateau/remain stable; peak at / reach a low of. Pattern: "increased sharply" = "a sharp increase".
- Degree: slightly/gradually/steadily/moderately (small) vs significantly/considerably/substantially/markedly/sharply/dramatically (large).
- Comparison: "twice as high as", "the highest figure", "compared with / whereas / while", "accounted for the largest proportion".
- Approximation: approximately / just over / nearly / in the region of / stood at / reached a peak of.
- **Process** (passive for manufacturing): "The glass is collected, then crushed, before being melted." Overview = number of stages + linear/cycle + start/end. Use present simple; sequence with varied markers, embedding some ("having been dried, it is then fired").
- **Maps** (past/past-perfect passive + compass directions): "was converted into / replaced by / demolished / relocated / underwent expansion"; "in the north-east, on the outskirts". Overview = overall transformation.

**Tense by task.** Past simple for finished dates; present simple for timeless processes/proportions; "is projected/expected to" for forecasts; past/past-perfect for maps.

**Common mistakes.** No or vague overview; putting numbers in the overview; data-dumping instead of selecting/grouping; describing instead of comparing; over-using linkers (Band 6 marker); wrong/inconsistent tense; using trend verbs on a map/process; chasing "big" words over accurate collocation; writing under 150 (penalised) or over ~200 (steals Task 2 time); high-frequency article/plural/comparative errors.

## Writing Task 2 (Essay)

**What it is.** ≥250-word academic essay in ~40 min, worth two-thirds of Writing. Band 7+ essays typically land 270–310 words; padding past ~320 wastes proofreading time. Copied prompt words and recognised memorised language don't count toward the total.

**Five essay types (match structure to type — the #1 Task Response error is using the wrong one).**
1. **Opinion / agree–disagree** — pick one side (or clear partial agreement) and defend it.
2. **Discussion (both views + your opinion)** — one paragraph per view; **your opinion must appear in intro AND conclusion**.
3. **Advantages–disadvantages** — one each; if asked "outweigh", give a verdict in thesis and conclusion.
4. **Problem/cause–solution** — causes paragraph, then solutions that logically match the causes.
5. **Two-part / direct question** — answer each question in its own developed paragraph; never merge them.

**Structure template (4 paragraphs).** Intro = paraphrase + clear thesis previewing reasons (~40–50 words). Two body paragraphs, each **PEEL**: Point (one clear topic sentence) → Explain (mechanism) → Example (specific, plausible — real statistics not required) → Link back to the question. Develop **two ideas deeply** rather than four shallowly. Conclusion = restate position + summarise reasons; **no new ideas**.

**Criteria focus.** The 6→7 leap = clear position throughout + genuinely developed ideas (TR), one central topic per paragraph + natural linking (CC), accurate collocation over rare words (LR), and frequent error-free complex sentences (GRA).

**Useful academic language.**
- Opinion: "From my perspective…", "I am inclined to believe…", "It is my firm conviction that…" (avoid repeating "In my opinion").
- Add/contrast: Furthermore, Moreover; However, Conversely, Nevertheless, That said.
- Cause/result: As a consequence, This results in, Owing to this.
- Concession: Although, While it is true that…, Admittedly.
- Present views: "One school of thought suggests…", "Proponents/Critics argue…".
- Aim for ~5–10 well-placed cohesive devices total; lean on referencing (this, such, these measures) over a linker on every sentence.

**Common mistakes.** Partial/off-topic answers (omitting "give your own opinion", answering only half a two-part question) — the leading cause of sub-Band-7 TR; memorised templates (disregarded by examiners); mechanical over-use of connectors; under-developed lists of points; a position that shifts between intro and conclusion; mixing two arguments in one paragraph; mis-collocation from over-reaching vocabulary; under 250 words; informal tone/contractions.

**Recent topic themes (2025–2026).** Education (online vs traditional, vocational vs academic, free university, History vs Science), Technology/AI (generative AI in study/work, screen time vs outdoor play, print vs digital media), Environment/energy, Health (obesity, fast-food pricing), Work (four-day week, future of skills, remote work), Society/economy (cost of living and housing, advertising aimed at children). *Note: specific topic-frequency percentages circulate from a third-party tool (Cathoven); IELTS publishes no topic statistics — treat as indicative only.*

## Speaking

**Part 1 (4–5 min, ~12 questions on familiar topics).** Use **ARE**: Answer → Reason → Example. Target 2–4 sentences. *"Do you like cooking?" → "Yes, I genuinely enjoy it — I find it therapeutic after a long day, and lately I've been experimenting with Thai curries; my green curry last weekend turned out surprisingly well."*

**Part 2 (cue card; 1 min prep, speak 1–2 min).** In prep, jot **keyword notes only** (4–6), plus 1–2 strong vocab items — never full sentences. Open by addressing the topic directly, follow the bullets as a skeleton but **expand** with a story/comparison/feeling, and save the final bullet ("explain why…") as your closer. Use a **Past–Present–Future** frame to guarantee content; speak ~30–40 s per bullet and keep going until stopped (stopping at ~50 s caps fluency).

**Part 3 (4–5 min, abstract discussion — where bands are decided).** Use **PEEL** and shift from "I" to general/societal ("people tend to", "in many societies"). **Hedge and speculate** for Band 7–8: "It largely depends on…", "To some extent…", "It's highly likely that…", "Were the government to invest more…". Acknowledge a counter-view then give yours ("Some would argue X, but personally Y"). Buy thinking time without dead air: "That's a thought-provoking question — off the top of my head…".

**Techniques that move bands.**
- **Fluency**: replace "um/uh/like" with silent micro-pauses or thinking phrases. Shadow native audio daily to build rhythm and cut hesitation.
- **Paraphrase on the fly**: if a word won't come, describe it ("something that can keep going without harming the environment" for *sustainable*) — converts a gap into evidence of skill.
- **Extension**: never give one-line answers, even in Part 1.
- **Pronunciation**: focus on suprasegmentals — word/sentence stress, intonation, chunking. **Do not** slow down and over-enunciate (it destroys rhythm). Accent is irrelevant; only intelligibility matters.
- **Grammar/lexis**: mix accurate complex structures with clean simple ones; deploy 1–2 natural collocations/idioms per answer ("a steep learning curve", "strike a balance", "a double-edged sword", "broaden your horizons", "a blessing in disguise") — don't overload.

**Recent cue-card topics (2025–2026).** A person who inspires you; a useful technology/app (or one hard to use); a time a device broke down; an important email/message; a skill you've learned or want to learn; a memorable/disrupted journey; useful advice given or received; a community/environmental event; a problem you solved; a book or film that impressed you; a place/city you'd like to visit; a creative person you know. *(Note: the ~4-month topic-refresh cycle is community-observed, not officially published.)*

**Common mistakes.** One-word answers; obviously memorised/scripted chunks (detected and unrewarded); filler overload and a narrow connective set ("and…so…because"); over-slow over-enunciation; stopping Part 2 early; reading prep notes aloud; forcing misused "big" words; treating Part 3 like Part 1 (short personal answers instead of abstract/hedged ones); avoiding complex grammar entirely; excessive self-correction; faking an accent.

## Listening (warm-up emphasis)

**Format (unchanged).** 4 parts, 40 questions, **heard once**. Order of difficulty Part 1→4; **answers always follow the order of the recording** (if you miss one, jump forward). Accents are deliberately varied (British, Australian, NZ, North American). Spelling and singular/plural are marked; exceeding the stated word limit = wrong (hyphenated words count as one). No negative marking — **never leave a blank**.

**Six question types.** Multiple choice (single, or "choose TWO/THREE"); matching; plan/map/diagram labelling; form/note/table/flow-chart/summary completion; sentence completion; short-answer.

**Strategies.** **Predict before you listen** — in each gap mark the answer type per blank (number? name? plural? noun?) and brainstorm synonyms (the recording paraphrases). Read 2–3 questions ahead at all times. For **maps**, orient first (find "you are here", mark N-S-E-W / L-R, read given labels) then follow directional language. For **Part 3**, track who holds which opinion and where they agree/disagree. For **Part 4** (no mid-break), lock onto signpost words (firstly/however/as a result/to sum up) to know which question is next.

**Common traps.** **Self-correction** — a speaker gives a value then changes it ("It's 30 — actually 35"); the **second** value is the answer (cues: sorry/actually/I mean/or rather). **Distractors** — all MCQ/matching options are usually mentioned; wait for the one confirmed and not negated ("we were going to use X, but chose Y"). Confusing 13/30, 14/40; "double 5" = 55. On computer there is **no 10-minute transfer** — type as you go and use the ~2-minute review only to fix spelling/plurals.

**Warm-up routine.** The morning of a test/mock, do one fresh Part 1 + Part 4 (10–15 min) to tune your ear; active dictation of numbers, dates, prices and addresses is the fastest ear-tuner.

## Reading (warm-up emphasis)

**Format.** 3 passages, 40 questions, **60 minutes including time to record answers — no separate/extra transfer time** (unlike Listening). 2,150–2,750 words total; Passage 1 easiest, Passage 3 hardest (~12–14 questions each). Spelling/grammar marked; word limits strict; no negative marking.

**Eleven question types.** Multiple choice; True/False/Not Given; Yes/No/Not Given; matching information; matching headings; matching features; matching sentence endings; sentence completion; summary/note/table/flow-chart completion; diagram-label completion; short-answer. Most types follow passage order; **matching headings/information/features do NOT**.

**Pacing.** ~20 min/passage (≈1 min/question), or a graduated 15/20/25 split. **Hard rule: move on at the 20-minute mark** so you always reach Passage 3. Write answers straight onto the sheet/screen as you go. Skim first (1–3 min) for gist and per-paragraph topic, then scan for keywords (proper nouns > numbers/dates > unusual content words). Do non-ordered tasks (matching information/features) **last**, after the passage is familiar. Guess every remaining blank in the final 1–2 minutes.

**T/F/NG vs Y/N/NG traps (key).** **T/F/NG = facts in the text; Y/N/NG = the writer's opinions/claims** — confirm which before answering. Decision rule: agrees = TRUE/YES; **directly contradicts** = FALSE/NO; **text is silent or only partial** = NOT GIVEN. **Never use outside knowledge or infer "it must be true"** — that is the classic Not-Given error. Watch qualifiers: "mostly" swapped to "always" is a typical FALSE. Match **meaning, not words** ("failed to win" ≠ "won"). For matching headings, choose the paragraph's **main idea** (often first/last sentence), each heading used once.

**Warm-up routine.** One timed Passage 1 + one harder passage daily, on screen, drilling T/F/NG and matching-headings specifically (likely weak question types).

## Verification notes

- **Cambridge editions corrected**: research first recommended volumes 18 & 19; current latest are **20 (2025)** and **21 (2026)** — use 19/20/21.
- **Paper phase-out is firmer than "becoming default"**: officially IELTS will **no longer be offered on paper** from mid-2026 (most markets ~27 June 2026). A Writing-on-Paper hybrid exists in selected markets; Listening/Reading remain fully on-screen.
- **One Skill Retake**: confirmed computer-only, 60-day window, once per test, same mode, **not available in the USA**.
- **Pronunciation 9-band scale is NOT a 2025 change** — defined odd bands since August 2008. Band-7 logic (all of 6 + some of 8) is genuine and long-standing.
- **Anti-memorisation "Band 4 cap"** is reported coaching guidance, not an official published figure — the qualitative penalty for memorised content is real.
- **Topic-frequency percentages** trace to a third-party tool (Cathoven), not IELTS — indicative only. An unverifiable "examiner report ~30% of candidates" statistic was **dropped**.
- **Reading wording**: officially "60 minutes (including transfer time)" — no *separate/extra* transfer time, unlike Listening's +10 min on paper.
- **Unverified trend**: "fewer matching headings / more matching sentence endings" is not supported by authoritative sources — do not over-prepare for it.
