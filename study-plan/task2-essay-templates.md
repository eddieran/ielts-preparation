# Task 2 Essay Templates + Connective Word Bank

Two parts: (1) a structural skeleton for each of the 5 question types, and (2) an exhaustive, function-organized bank of connecting words with the grammar and punctuation rules.

> **Read this first.** These are **structural skeletons, not phrases to memorize.** Examiners are trained to spot and *disregard* memorized template language (it earns zero and signals a lower band). The paragraph *jobs* are fixed; you write the actual sentences fresh each time, varying wording from the bank below. And on connectives: **more is not better.** Aim for **~5–10 well-placed devices in the whole essay.** A linker on every sentence is a *Band 6 marker*. Lean on referencing (this/such/these) as much as on linkers.

---

# Part 1 — The 5 question types

## Universal building blocks (used in ALL types)

Every Task 2 essay = 4 paragraphs, ~270–310 words, ~40 min:

- **Intro (~40–50 words):** paraphrase the question (don't copy) + thesis (your position and/or a preview of two points).
- **2 body paragraphs, each PEEL:**
  - **P**oint — one clear topic sentence (the paragraph's single idea)
  - **E**xplain — *why/how* it's true (the mechanism) ← band-6 essays skip this
  - **E**xample — specific, plausible (invented is fine; real stats not required)
  - **L**ink — one sentence back to the question
- **Conclusion (~40 words):** restate position + summarize the two points. No new ideas.

Develop **two ideas deeply**, not four shallowly.

---

## 1. Opinion / Agree or Disagree

**Triggers:** "To what extent do you agree or disagree?" · "Do you agree or disagree?" · "What is your opinion?"

**Structure (pick ONE position, hold it throughout):**
- Intro: paraphrase + clear position + preview 2 reasons
- Body 1: PEEL on reason 1 (your side)
- Body 2: PEEL on reason 2 (your side)
- Conclusion: restate position + reasons

```
Intro:      [Paraphrase claim]. I [completely agree / firmly disagree] that
            [position], primarily because [reason 1] and [reason 2].
Body 1:     [Reason 1 = topic sentence]. [Explain why]. [Example]. [Link]
Body 2:     [Reason 2 = topic sentence]. [Explain why]. [Example]. [Link]
Conclusion: [Restate position] because [reason 1] and [reason 2].
```

**Variant — partial agreement:** thesis says so ("I agree only to an extent..."), Body 1 concedes the other side, Body 2 argues your stronger side. Either works, but **commit to a clear stance** — a wobbling position kills Task Response.

---

## 2. Discussion (both views + your opinion)

**Trigger:** "Discuss both views **and give your own opinion**."
**#1 mistake:** forgetting the opinion. It **must appear in the intro AND conclusion.**

- Intro: paraphrase + both views exist + which you favor
- Body 1: the view you disagree with — explained fairly (PEEL)
- Body 2: the view you agree with — explained + endorsed (PEEL)
- Conclusion: restate which view is more convincing to you

```
Intro:      [Paraphrase]. While some argue [view A], others believe [view B].
            This essay discusses both, though I side with [your view].
Body 1:     On the one hand, [view A]. [Why people hold it]. [Example]. [Link]
Body 2:     On the other hand, [view B], which I find more convincing. [Why].
            [Example]. [Link]
Conclusion: Having weighed both, I believe [your view] because [reason].
```

---

## 3. Advantages and Disadvantages

**Read the prompt — two sub-types need different handling:**
- **(a) "What are the advantages and disadvantages?"** → neutral, describe both, no verdict needed.
- **(b) "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?"** → **must give a verdict** in thesis AND conclusion.

- Intro: paraphrase + (for "outweigh") state which side wins
- Body 1: advantages (PEEL)
- Body 2: disadvantages (PEEL)
- Conclusion: (for "outweigh") restate verdict

```
Intro:      [Paraphrase]. Although this has drawbacks, I believe its benefits
            are more significant overall.
Body 1:     The main advantage is [adv]. [Explain]. [Example]. [Link]
Body 2:     On the downside, [disadv]. [Explain]. [Example, + why outweighed]. [Link]
Conclusion: Despite [disadvantage], the advantages of [topic] clearly outweigh
            them because [reason].
```

---

## 4. Problem/Cause → Solution

**Triggers:** "What problems does this cause and what solutions...?" · "What are the causes... and what measures...?"

- Intro: paraphrase + signal causes/problems + solutions
- Body 1: causes / problems (PEEL)
- Body 2: solutions that **logically match** those causes (PEEL)
- Conclusion: summary

```
Intro:      [Paraphrase]. This essay examines the main [causes/problems] and
            proposes realistic solutions.
Body 1:     A key [cause/problem] is [X]. [Explain]. [Example]. [Link]
Body 2:     A practical solution to this is [Y, addressing X]. [How it helps].
            [Example]. [Link]
Conclusion: In short, [problem] stems largely from [cause], which could be
            tackled by [solution].
```

**Key:** if Body 1's cause is "cheap fast food," Body 2's solution must connect ("taxing high-sugar products"). Mismatched cause/solution = weak Task Response.

---

## 5. Two-part / Direct questions

**Trigger:** two distinct questions, e.g. *"Why is this happening? Is it a positive or negative development?"*

- Intro: paraphrase + briefly answer both
- Body 1: fully answer question 1
- Body 2: fully answer question 2
- Conclusion: recap both

```
Intro:      [Paraphrase]. This is mainly due to [brief answer Q1], and in my
            view it is a [positive/negative] development.
Body 1:     [Answer Q1 — the "why"]. [Explain]. [Example]. [Link]
Body 2:     [Answer Q2 — your evaluation]. [Explain]. [Example]. [Link]
Conclusion: In conclusion, [recap Q1] and [recap Q2].
```

**Key:** answer **each question separately and fully** — never merge or skip one.

# Part 2 — The connective word bank (exhaustive)

## First: the grammar of connectives (this is where GRA marks are won or lost)

Three different grammatical groups — mixing them up is a classic error that caps your band:

| Group | Examples | How to use it | ✗ Common error |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conjunctions** (join clauses inside one sentence) | and, but, so, because, since, as, although, even though, while, whereas, unless, if | `[clause], [conjunction] [clause]` — no full stop/semicolon before | ✗ Starting a sentence with "Because..." as a fragment |
| **Linking adverbials** (connect two sentences) | however, therefore, moreover, nevertheless, consequently, furthermore, thus | New sentence + comma: `... . However, ...` OR after a semicolon: `... ; however, ...` | ✗ "It rained, however we left." (comma splice — needs `. However,`) |
| **Prepositions / prepositional phrases** (take a noun/gerund, NOT a clause) | despite, in spite of, due to, owing to, because of | `[prep] + noun/gerund`: "Despite the rain..." / "Despite being tired..." | ✗ "Despite it rained" — must be "Despite the rain" or "Although it rained" |

**The three deadliest pairings to avoid:**
- ✗ "**Although** ... , **but** ..." — use only ONE. (Although X, Y. *not* Although X, but Y.)
- ✗ "**Despite** it rained" — Despite + noun ("Despite the rain") or switch to "Although it rained."
- ✗ "**Because** of it is cheap" — "Because it is cheap" (clause) OR "Because of its low cost" (noun).

## Adding / listing more information

| Use | Words & phrases | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Add a point | Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Additionally, Besides, What is more, Also, Apart from this, On top of this | Sentence-start + comma. *Furthermore/Moreover* are the most formal. |
| Add within a sentence | as well as, along with, coupled with, in addition to, not only ... but also ... | "Not only ... but also ..." can invert for emphasis: "Not only does it save money, but it also..." |
| Sequence | First / First of all / To begin with, Second(ly), Third(ly), Next, Then, Finally, Lastly | Don't mechanically stack "Firstly... Secondly... Moreover..." — that's a Band 6 tell. |

## Contrast & concession

| Use | Words & phrases | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Contrast two sentences | However, Nevertheless, Nonetheless, In contrast, By contrast, Conversely, On the other hand | Sentence-start + comma. |
| Contrast within a sentence | whereas, while | Direct comparison: "City A grew, whereas City B declined." |
| Concession (clause) | although, even though, though | + clause: "Although costs are high, ..." |
| Concession (noun) | despite, in spite of, notwithstanding | + noun/gerund: "Despite the cost, ..." |
| Soft concession | That said, Having said that, Admittedly, It is true that, Granted, Of course, At the same time, Yet | Use to concede a point before countering it (great for discussion essays). |
| Correcting a statement | On the contrary | ≠ "on the other hand." Use *only* to negate what was just said: "It is not harmful; on the contrary, it helps." |

## Cause / reason

| Form | Words & phrases | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| + clause | because, since, as, given that, seeing that, now that | "Since demand is rising, ..." |
| + noun/gerund | due to, owing to, because of, on account of, as a result of | "Owing to rising demand, ..." |
| Phrases | The main reason for this is..., This stems from..., This is largely attributable to..., This can be traced back to... | Good for topic sentences. |

## Effect / result / consequence

| Words & phrases | Notes |
|---|---|
| Therefore, Thus, Hence, Consequently, As a result, As a consequence, Accordingly, For this reason | Sentence-start + comma; or after a semicolon. *Thus/Hence* are the most formal. |
| This leads to..., This results in..., This gives rise to..., This means that..., This is why... | Within or across sentences. "leads to / results in" + noun. |

## Purpose

`in order to`, `so as to`, `so that`, `with the aim of (+ gerund)`, `with a view to (+ gerund)`, `for the purpose of (+ gerund)` — e.g. "...with a view to reducing emissions."

## Condition

`if`, `unless`, `provided (that)`, `providing`, `as long as`, `on condition that`, `in case` — plus inversions for a band-8 flourish: **"Should governments invest more, ..."** / **"Were this policy adopted, ..."**

## Giving examples

`For example,` · `For instance,` · `To illustrate,` · `such as (+ noun)` · `namely,` · `in particular,` · `particularly` · `A case in point is...` · `As an illustration,`

## Comparison / similarity

`Similarly,` · `Likewise,` · `In the same way,` · `Equally,` · `Correspondingly,` · `By the same token,` · `Just as ... , so too ...`

## Emphasis

`Indeed,` · `In fact,` · `Clearly,` · `Undoubtedly,` · `Notably,` · `Importantly,` · `Above all,` · `Especially,` · `It is worth noting that...`

## Generalizing

`In general,` · `Generally,` · `On the whole,` · `By and large,` · `For the most part,` · `In most cases,` · `As a rule,` · `Broadly speaking,` · `Overall,`

## Clarifying / rephrasing

`In other words,` · `That is to say,` · `To put it another way,` · `Namely,` · `To clarify,`

## Stating your opinion (vary these — don't repeat "In my opinion")

`In my view,` · `From my perspective,` · `I would argue that...` · `I am inclined to believe that...` · `It seems to me that...` · `As far as I am concerned,` · `To my mind,` · `I firmly believe that...` · `Personally, I feel that...`

## Presenting other people's views

`It is often argued that...` · `Some people claim / maintain / contend that...` · `Proponents argue that...` · `Critics contend that...` · `One school of thought suggests that...` · `It is widely believed that...` · `There is a common perception that...`

## Summarizing / concluding

`In conclusion,` · `To conclude,` · `In summary,` · `To sum up,` · `On balance,` · `Taking everything into account,` · `Ultimately,` · `Weighing up both sides,` · `In the final analysis,`

## Referencing — the "invisible" cohesion that scores Band 7

Band 7 Coherence is about *natural* flow, not linker density. Replace some connectors with **referencing** so the essay knits together without "Moreover" on every line:

- **this / these / that / those + noun:** *this trend, these measures, such policies, this phenomenon, this approach, these factors*
- **the former / the latter** (to refer back to two things mentioned)
- **doing so / by doing so** ("Governments could subsidise transport; by doing so, they would...")
- **such (a) + noun** ("such a policy", "such concerns")
- **pronouns:** it, they, which, this

---

## Use sparingly or avoid (too informal / overused for academic writing)

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| a lot of / lots of | a great deal of, numerous, a significant number of |
| plus, and stuff, and so on, etc. | (finish the thought, or "and similar measures") |
| Last but not least | Finally, / Lastly, |
| basically, actually, kind of, really | (delete — usually filler) |
| anyways, so... (informal "so") | Therefore, / Consequently, |
| big / huge / good / bad | substantial, considerable, beneficial, detrimental |
| contractions (don't, it's, can't) | do not, it is, cannot |

## The 6 rules for using all of this

1. **Identify the question type in the first 30 seconds** — wrong structure = the most common Task Response failure.
2. Use the skeleton for paragraph *jobs*; write the sentences yourself.
3. **~5–10 connectives per essay, varied.** Over-linking signals Band 6.
4. Mix linkers with **referencing** (this/such/these/the former).
5. Get the **grammar group right** (clause vs noun vs sentence-link) — that's GRA marks.
6. These same connectives lift your **Speaking** band too (Band 7 needs "a range of connectives used flexibly") — rehearse them aloud, not just on paper.
