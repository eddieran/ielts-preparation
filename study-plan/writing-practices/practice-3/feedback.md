## The 3 things costing you the most (priority order)

### 1. ❌ Grammatical accuracy — the only Band 5, so it's the #1 target

Three error types recur in nearly every sentence. Fix these and GRA jumps to 6.

**(a) Comma splices / run-on sentences** — two complete sentences joined by a comma. Your concession is one giant run-on:
> "…human have been survived for thousands of years**,** there is no systematic training for childcare**,** nevertheless**,** we are living in modern society…"

That's **four** independent clauses comma-spliced together. Each needs a full stop or a proper linker. Same fault in the conclusion ("…not capable of choosing**,** we should take…"). **The single fastest GRA win: when two ideas can each stand alone, use a full stop, not a comma.**

**(b) Missing articles (a / an / the)** — pervasive:
- "different from **adult**" → "from **an** adult" (or "from **that of an** adult")
- "for **whole** society" → "for **the whole of** society"
- "taught by **doctor**" → "taught by **a** doctor"
- "is most important thing" → "is **the** most important thing"

**(c) Subject-verb agreement:**
- "children **is** most important" → "children **are**"
- "knowledge … **are** broadly known" → "knowledge … **is**"
- "the arguments … **leads** me" → "**lead**"
- "human **have been survived**" → "humans **have survived**" (survive isn't passive)

### 2. ❌ Spelling — different words from last time, same lost marks

**infrant** (×2 → *infant*), **boardly** (→ *broadly*), **tought** (→ *taught*), **arguements** (→ *arguments*), "an birth"/"an clear" (→ *a*). Add these to the spelling list you started in practice-2. Frequent spelling errors hold LR at 5.5.

### 3. ❌ Two repeat offenders from practice-2's action list

- **Copying the prompt.** Your conclusion lifts "all mothers and fathers should be required to take childcare training courses" word-for-word from the question. Copied words don't count and signal weak LR. Paraphrase: "prospective parents should be obliged to undertake professional childcare instruction."
- **New idea in the conclusion.** "children are not capable of choosing" appears for the first time in the last sentence. Conclusions only *restate* — never introduce a new argument.

Plus one new content issue: the **example's units are wrong.** "5–10 milligrams" / "stomach is around 15 milligrams" — milligrams measure *weight*, not liquid volume. A newborn's stomach holds about **5–7 millilitres** (ml). Examiners don't fact-check, but "milligrams" of milk reads as a lexical error.

## Band 7 model answer — *your essay, fixed*

Same two arguments (infant fragility + specialised health knowledge; children modelling their parents), same concession, same agree stance. Only the execution changed. (~300 words; independently verified at Band 7.0–7.5.)

```
Few responsibilities matter more to a society than the upbringing of its
children, and some argue that every parent ought to complete a formal childcare
course before raising a child. I largely agree with this view, mainly because
nurturing a child in a healthy way, both mentally and physically, is far harder
than most people assume, and first-time parents in particular have a great deal
of knowledge and skills to acquire.

The most compelling reason for agreement is that infants are fragile and
vulnerable, especially in terms of health and communication. Because a baby's
body is structured so differently from an adult's, the everyday common sense
that adults broadly rely on is simply not suitable for a newborn. For example,
an infant needs to be fed every two to three hours with only about 5 to 7
millilitres of milk, since a newborn's stomach is no larger than a cherry at
birth. Such specialised knowledge is exactly what a structured course would
ensure every parent receives, rather than leaving it to a rushed post-natal
briefing.

A second, equally important consideration is children's mental and emotional
development. As they grow, children do not only gain factual knowledge at
school; more crucially, they learn empathy, emotional stability and critical
thinking from their parents. The way mothers and fathers react to daily
situations is observed, absorbed and repeated by their children over and over
again. Admittedly, opponents point out that humanity has survived for thousands
of years without any systematic childcare training. Nevertheless, we now live in
a modern society that has the means to raise children far better than before,
drawing on paediatric research and accessible training that earlier generations
simply lacked.

In conclusion, the points discussed above lead me to agree that prospective
mothers and fathers should be obliged to undertake some form of professional
childcare instruction, so that they can shoulder the demanding responsibilities
of parenthood with proper preparation.
```

**Why this scores ~7 (and what changed):**
- **Grammar cleaned:** every comma splice broken into proper sentences, every missing article restored, agreement fixed ("children are", "arguments lead", "humanity has survived"). *(GRA 5 → 7 — the move that matters most)*
- **Parallelism repaired:** "not only … most importantly" → "do not only … more crucially, they…". *(GRA)*
- **Example fixed:** correct unit and a vivid comparison ("5 to 7 millilitres … no larger than a cherry"), and reframed so it *supports* the need for a course. *(TR + LR)*
- **Conclusion restates only** — the "children can't choose" new idea is gone, and the prompt wording is paraphrased. *(TR)*
- **Spelling clean:** infant, broadly, taught, arguments. *(LR)*

## Your action list (GRA is the whole game now)
