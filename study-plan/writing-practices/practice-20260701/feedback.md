## The honest headline: you answered a question that wasn't asked

**This is a Task Response problem, and it's the most costly kind — because the question analysis you did before writing had already identified the two parts correctly: ① Is this good? ② What are the consequences?** Knowing the right structure and then writing a different one is a habit problem, not a knowledge problem — which is exactly why it's fixable fast.

But your essay's roadmap says: *"The following paragraphs will first explain **why the trend is occurring**"* — and your entire second paragraph ("A significant driver is that people are making more money…") answers **why parents do this**. That question was **never asked.**

You pulled the wrong template. In [`task2-sentence-frames.md` §5](../../task2-sentence-frames.md) there are two flavours of two-part question:
- *"**Why** is this happening? Is it positive/negative?"* → causes + evaluation
- *"**Is this good?** What are the consequences?"* → evaluation + consequences ← **this one**

You wrote the first structure for the second prompt. The cost: ~25% of your essay (Body 1) is off-task, so your actual answers to "is it good?" and "what consequences?" are squeezed into one crowded Body 2 plus the conclusion. That single misread pulled TR from 6 down to 5, and with it the whole band.

**The good news is unchanged from practice-3:** your position is clear and consistent (negative/"disastrous"), your paragraph structure is sound, and your real ideas (children won't value money or effort, they become fragile adults, they're disappointed when the world won't indulge them) are genuinely good. You just spent a paragraph answering the wrong question.

## Band 7.5 model answer — *your ideas, right question*

Same negative stance, same consequence ideas (never valuing effort, fragile adults, chronic disappointment). The difference: it answers the **two asked questions** and cuts the causes paragraph. (288 words; independently verified at Band 7.5.)

```
In many households today, parents indulge their children by handing them
whatever they request and permitting them to behave exactly as they please. In
my view, this approach is not good for children, and it is likely to produce
adults who struggle to withstand the demands of everyday life.

This is harmful to children in two concrete ways. First, young children lack the
maturity to regulate their own desires: at an early age, they cannot yet judge
what is sensible, what is safe, or how much things actually cost. When parents
give in to every demand, they fail to teach essential limits and sound
judgement, and they deprive their children of any sense of the value of money
and effort. Second, rather than learning that rewards must be earned, a child
simply learns that wishing for something is enough to obtain it. Discipline and
patience, which are best developed in childhood, are therefore never
established.

The consequences of such an upbringing become clear once these children reach
adulthood. Because everything was handed to them, they never understood how hard
their parents worked to raise the family, and they grow up taking everything for
granted. As a result, they tend to be fragile and unable to cope with the
pressures of adult life, from demanding careers to the responsibility of raising
their own families. Furthermore, the wider world will not indulge them as their
parents once did, so they are repeatedly disappointed whenever they cannot obtain
what they want with ease.

In conclusion, indulging a child's every wish is far from beneficial: it harms
children by leaving key qualities undeveloped, and it produces fragile adults
poorly equipped for the disappointments that life inevitably brings.
```

**Why this scores ~7.5 (and what changed):**
- **Task Response fixed (the big one):** the off-task "parents make more money" causes paragraph is **gone**. Body 1 now answers "**is it good?**" (no) and Body 2 answers "**what consequences?**" — the two questions actually asked. *(TR 5 → 7)*
- **Both answers in the intro:** "not good for children" + "adults who struggle…" — the reader knows your two-part answer immediately. *(TR)*
- **Grammar rebuilt:** broken opening frame, the "Because…" fragment, and the "If…, which…" break are all now complete sentences; articles and agreement corrected. *(GRA 5 → 7)*
- **Collocations upgraded:** "feel easy to everything" → "taking everything for granted"; "fragile to take the pressure" → "unable to cope with the pressures of adult life." *(LR)*
- **Spelling + register clean:** every misspelling fixed, contractions removed. *(LR)*
