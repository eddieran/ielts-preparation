# Task 2 — Fillable Sentence Frames + Worked Examples

**Companion to [task2-essay-templates.md](task2-essay-templates.md).** That file gives you the paragraph *skeletons* (the job each paragraph does) and the exhaustive connective word bank. **This file goes one level more concrete:** ready fill-in-the-blank sentences for every paragraph job, with **2–3 interchangeable variants each**, plus a complete **Band 8–9 worked essay** for every question type.

---

> ### Read this first — how to use these without getting penalized
>
> Examiners are trained to spot **memorized template language** and *disregard* it (it scores 0 for that content and signals a lower band). So these frames are deliberately given as **2–3 variants per slot** — your job is to **vary**, not parrot. Three rules:
>
> 1. **Fill every `[bracket]`** with your own words. The brackets tell you *what kind of thing* goes there (e.g. `[reason 1 as a noun phrase]`, `[restate the claim in your own words]`) — never leave a bracket in your final essay.
> 2. **Rotate the variants.** Don't use frame 1 of every slot in every essay; mix 1/2/3 across slots so two of your essays never read identically. Reword the *tail* of a frame (the bit after the comma) most of all — that's what examiners recognize fastest.
> 3. **Pick the right type in the first 30 seconds.** Using the wrong structure is the single most common Task Response failure. Use the identification table below.

## 30-second identification table

| If the prompt says… | Type | The non-negotiable obligation |
|---|---|---|
| "To what extent do you agree or disagree?" · "What is your opinion?" | **§1 Opinion** | One consistent stance from intro to conclusion; on "to what extent" signal *degree* (largely / in part). |
| "Discuss both views **and give your own opinion**." | **§2 Discussion** | Your opinion must appear in **both** the intro and the conclusion. |
| "What are the advantages and disadvantages?" | **§3 Adv/Dis — neutral** | Describe both sides; **no verdict**. |
| "Do the advantages **outweigh** the disadvantages?" | **§3 Adv/Dis — outweigh** | A clear **verdict in thesis AND conclusion**. |
| "What problems… what solutions?" · "What causes… what measures?" | **§4 Problem → Solution** | Body 2 must solve the **exact** problem/cause raised in Body 1. |
| Two questions, e.g. "Why…? Is it positive or negative?" | **§5 Two-part** | Answer **both** questions, fully and separately. |

> **The universal 4-paragraph shape (all types):** Intro (~40–50 w: paraphrase + thesis) → Body 1 (PEEL) → Body 2 (PEEL) → Conclusion (~40 w: restate, no new ideas). Aim ~270–310 words in ~40 min. **Develop two ideas deeply, not four shallowly.**

## 1. Opinion / Agree or Disagree

**When you see it:** The prompt makes a single claim and then asks how far you accept it — "To what extent do you agree or disagree?", "Do you agree or disagree?", or "What is your opinion?" Your job is to take a clear, consistent position and defend it, not to survey both sides neutrally.

**Structure at a glance:** Four paragraphs. **Intro** paraphrases the claim and states your position with a two-reason preview; **Body 1** develops your strongest reason (claim → explain → example → link); **Body 2** develops a second reason *or* concedes-and-rebuts the opposing side; **Conclusion** restates the position and recaps the reasons.

> Choose ONE of two approaches before you write: **(a) one-sided** — pick a side and support it with two reasons; or **(b) balanced / partial agreement** — concede a genuine point to the other side, then argue your stronger side. The thesis and Body 2 frames below are split accordingly.

### Fillable sentence frames

#### Introduction — paraphrase
1. It is frequently argued that [restate the prompt's central proposition as a noun clause, using synonyms].
2. In recent debate, the issue of whether [reword the claim's key nouns and verbs] has become increasingly prominent.
3. A widely held view is that [paraphrase the statement so that no more than two words overlap with the original].

> Note: "It is frequently argued that" (frame 1) is one of the most over-used openers in IELTS scripts. Reword it — for example, "The question of whether [reworded claim] has become a prominent point of debate" — so it does not read as a memorized stock phrase.

#### Introduction — thesis / position — *one-sided approach*
1. I broadly agree with this view, chiefly because of [reason 1 as a noun phrase] and [reason 2 as a noun phrase].
2. In my opinion, this assertion is largely [convincing / unfounded], primarily because [reason 1 as a full clause] and, more importantly, [reason 2 as a full clause].
3. On the whole, I am inclined to [endorse / reject] this position, both on the grounds that [first supporting idea as a clause] and because [second supporting idea as a clause].

> Calibrate the strength of the verb to the prompt. For a blunt **"Do you agree or disagree?"** prompt you may commit more absolutely (e.g. *"I wholeheartedly endorse this view, without reservation"*). On a **"To what extent…"** prompt, always signal *degree* — *"broadly", "largely", "on the whole", "agree only in part"* — so your thesis answers the scale the question sets rather than ignoring it.

#### Introduction — thesis / position — *balanced / partial-agreement approach*
1. While I accept that [concede the opposing point as a noun phrase], I largely agree that [restate your stronger side], chiefly because of [your dominant reason].
2. Although [acknowledge a legitimate point on the other side] carries some weight, I am inclined to [agree / disagree], since [your decisive reason].
3. This essay concedes that [the opposing consideration]; nevertheless, it contends that [your overall position], as [the reason your side prevails].

#### Body paragraph 1 — topic sentence
1. The most compelling reason for [agreeing / disagreeing] is that [reason 1 expressed as a full clause].
2. The principal argument in favour of [your position] concerns [reason 1 as a noun phrase].
3. Foremost among the justifications for this stance is [reason 1 as a noun phrase].

#### Body — explanation move ("explain why")
1. This is because [spell out the cause-and-effect mechanism in your own words].
2. The underlying reason is that [explain the logic linking the reason to your position], which means that [logical consequence].
3. To put it differently, [restate the mechanism], and as a result [the outcome it produces].

#### Body — example move
1. [Country / institution / group], for instance, [describe the specific case that illustrates the point].
2. A clear illustration of this can be seen in [specific example], where [the outcome that supports your claim].
3. This is well demonstrated by [concrete example], which shows that [the conclusion you draw from it].

#### Body — link-back sentence
1. This example confirms that [tie the evidence back to your topic sentence and position].
2. Such cases make it clear that [restate the paragraph's claim in the light of the evidence].
3. It therefore follows that [the mini-conclusion that reinforces your thesis].

#### Body paragraph 2 — topic sentence
> *Difference for this type:* in a **one-sided** essay this opens a second, independent reason for the *same* position; in a **balanced** essay it concedes the opposing view and then rebuts it.

*One-sided (second reason):*
1. A second, equally important consideration is that [reason 2 as a full clause].
2. Beyond this, [your position] is further justified by [reason 2 as a noun phrase].
3. Equally significant is the fact that [reason 2 as a full clause].

*Balanced (concede-and-rebut):*
1. Admittedly, those who take the opposite view argue that [the opposing argument]; nevertheless, [your rebuttal that re-establishes your position].
2. It would be unreasonable to deny that [the valid opposing point]. However, this concern is outweighed by [the reason your side prevails].
3. There is, admittedly, force in the claim that [opposing point]; even so, [rebuttal that restores the thesis].

#### Conclusion
1. In conclusion, although [brief nod to the other side], I remain convinced that [restate your position], owing to [summarise your two reasons].
2. To summarise, the arguments presented above lead me to [agree / disagree] that [restate the proposition], primarily because of [reason 1] and [reason 2].
3. On balance, I firmly believe that [restate your overall position]; [paraphrase your strongest reason] makes this the more defensible conclusion.

### Worked example

> Some people believe that university education should be free for all students, regardless of their financial background. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

**[Intro — paraphrase + thesis (balanced / partial agreement)]**
The question of whether higher education should be funded entirely by the state, regardless of a student's means, has become a prominent point of debate. While the cost of implementing such a policy is undeniably significant, I largely agree with this view, since the resulting gains in social equality and long-term national prosperity outweigh the financial burden.

**[Body 1 — topic sentence (strongest reason) → explain → example → link]**
The principal justification for abolishing tuition fees is that it removes the most formidable barrier to social mobility. When access to a degree depends on a family's wealth, talented individuals from disadvantaged backgrounds are effectively excluded from the professions, and society is deprived of their potential. By contrast, a free system ensures that admission is determined by ability rather than affluence. Finland, for instance, charges no tuition and consistently produces highly skilled graduates drawn from across the social spectrum, which demonstrates how state funding can widen opportunity.

**[Body 2 — concede-and-rebut → restore thesis]**
The most serious objection to this policy is financial: a fully state-funded system imposes a considerable burden on taxpayers, much of which falls on those who never attend university themselves. This is a legitimate concern that no honest defence of free education can ignore. Nevertheless, it overlooks the return such investment generates. Graduates in fields such as medicine, engineering and teaching provide services upon which the entire population depends, and the higher taxes they subsequently pay tend to recoup the initial outlay many times over. The cost is therefore real but recoverable, whereas the cost of leaving talent untapped is permanent.

**[Conclusion — restate position + recap reasons]**
In conclusion, although a free higher-education system undeniably entails substantial public spending, I am firmly convinced that the advantages it brings, in terms of both fairer access for the disadvantaged and stronger long-term economic returns, render it a worthwhile and thoroughly defensible policy that governments should prioritise.

### Watch out for
- **Do not sit on the fence.** A "to what extent" prompt still demands one consistent stance from the first sentence to the last; a neutral 50/50 discussion with no commitment is the most common way to cap your score.
- **Answer the scale, not just the verb.** If the question says "to what extent," let your thesis signal *degree* — "largely agree," "agree only in part" — rather than a flat "I agree" that ignores how the question is framed.
- **Keep the two reasons genuinely distinct.** Two body paragraphs that reword the same idea read as one point padded out; in a partial-agreement essay, make sure the concession is actually *rebutted*, not left hanging where it quietly undermines your own case.
- **Avoid vague evidence.** Replace "studies show" or "many people" with a named country, institution, or concrete scenario, so the example is specific and instructive rather than generic filler.

## 2. Discussion (both views + your opinion)

**When you see it:** The prompt sets out two opposing positions on an issue and instructs you to "Discuss both views and give your own opinion." You are required to examine each side fairly *and* commit to a clear stance of your own — not merely sit on the fence.

**Structure at a glance:** **Intro** paraphrases the debate and states your position. **Body 1** presents the view you find *less* convincing, explained fairly and sympathetically. **Body 2** presents and *endorses* the view you side with, with stronger development. **Conclusion** restates both sides briefly and re-asserts your opinion. Your stance must surface in *both* the introduction and the conclusion.

### Fillable sentence frames

#### Introduction — paraphrase
1. It is often argued that [paraphrase view A as a noun clause], whereas others maintain that [paraphrase view B as a noun clause].
2. Opinion is divided over whether [restate the central question as a "whether" clause], or whether the opposite is true: [restate the competing possibility].
3. The question of [topic as a noun phrase] has prompted considerable debate, with some favouring [view A in a few words] and others advocating [view B in a few words].

#### Introduction — thesis / position
1. While both standpoints have merit, this essay will argue that [state the view you endorse, in your own words] is the more compelling position.
2. On balance, I am persuaded that [your endorsed claim as a full clause].
3. This essay will consider each argument in turn before contending that [your position as a clause] ultimately carries greater weight.

#### Body paragraph 1 (present view A — the less convincing side) — topic sentence
1. Those who believe that [view A as a clause] tend to justify their position by pointing to [the basis of the argument as a noun phrase].
2. There is, admittedly, a respectable case to be made for [view A in your own words].
3. Advocates of [view A in a few words] argue that [the core reasoning behind view A].

#### Body — explanation move ("explain why")
1. The reasoning here is that [spell out the causal logic, as a full clause], which they regard as a decisive advantage.
2. This view rests on the assumption that [state the underlying assumption], and from this it follows that [the consequence they draw].
3. According to this line of thought, [restate the mechanism], thereby [the benefit they claim].

#### Body — example move
1. A frequently cited illustration is [a concrete, realistic example], where [briefly state what the example shows].
2. This is evident in the case of [specific instance], in which [the relevant outcome].
3. Supporters often point, for instance, to [example], as confirmation that [the point being supported].

#### Body — link-back sentence
1. For these reasons, the appeal of [view A in a few words] is understandable.
2. Such considerations lend this perspective a degree of legitimacy that should not be dismissed.
3. It is therefore easy to see why this argument commands a measure of support.

#### Body paragraph 2 (present view B + endorse it) — topic sentence
*Note: unlike Body 1, this paragraph signals your endorsement and is developed more persuasively — concede A briefly, then pivot to the stronger merits of B.*
1. Nevertheless, I find the opposing argument considerably more persuasive, namely that [view B as a clause].
2. While the preceding case is not without value, the more convincing position holds that [restate view B], for several reasons.
3. The view I ultimately endorse, however, is that [view B as a clause], which addresses concerns the first argument overlooks.

#### Conclusion
1. In conclusion, although [brief nod to view A] carries some weight, the balance of argument favours [restate view B], and it is this position that I support.
2. To summarise, both perspectives merit consideration, yet I remain convinced that [restate your endorsed view] offers the more defensible conclusion.
3. Weighing the two standpoints, I conclude that, despite the strengths of [view A in a few words], [your position] is, on balance, the sounder view.

### Worked example

> Some people believe that children should begin formal schooling at a very early age, while others think they should start later. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.

**[Intro — paraphrase + thesis/position]**
Opinion is divided over whether children benefit from entering formal education as young as possible or whether a later start serves them better. While the case for early schooling is not without foundation, this essay will argue that delaying formal instruction is the more compelling position, as it better accommodates how young children actually develop.

**[Body 1 — present view A, the less convincing side]**
Those who advocate an early start tend to justify their position by pointing to the supposed advantages of a head start in literacy and numeracy. The reasoning here is that the brain is exceptionally receptive in the first years of life, and from this it follows that structured teaching during this window yields lasting academic gains. Illustrations are frequently drawn from countries with rigorous early curricula, where children can read fluently well before their peers elsewhere. For these reasons, the appeal of beginning school early is understandable.

**[Body 2 — present view B + endorse it]**
Nevertheless, I find the opposing argument considerably more persuasive, namely that an early academic emphasis can prove counterproductive. The danger is that imposing formal demands prematurely deprives children of unstructured play, through which they cultivate creativity, social competence and emotional resilience. This is evident in the case of several Scandinavian systems, in which formal instruction is deferred until around the age of seven, yet long-term educational outcomes remain among the strongest in the world. Such evidence suggests that early literacy advantages tend to level out, whereas the developmental costs of premature pressure may endure. A later start, therefore, addresses needs that an exclusively academic approach overlooks.

**[Conclusion — restate both sides + re-assert opinion]**
In conclusion, although the argument for early schooling rightly recognises the receptiveness of young minds, the balance of evidence favours allowing children to mature before formal education begins. It is this position that I support, since a developmentally appropriate start is more likely to produce well-rounded and capable learners.

### Watch out for

- **Do not stay neutral.** "Discuss both views" still demands a personal opinion; an essay that weighs both sides without committing will be capped on Task Response. State your position in the intro *and* the conclusion.
- **Keep your stance consistent.** The view you endorse in Body 2 must be the same one named in your thesis and conclusion — examiners penalise a position that drifts mid-essay.
- **Treat the "less convincing" side fairly.** Body 1 should explain view A sympathetically, not strawman it. A fair concession before you pivot makes your endorsement of view B look more reasoned, not less.
- **Mind the three connective groups.** Use *whereas/while* as conjunctions (joining clauses), *however/nevertheless* as linking adverbials (with a full stop or semicolon before, comma after), and *despite/in spite of* as prepositions (followed by a noun phrase, never a clause). Mixing these is a common Band-ceiling grammar error.

## 3. Advantages and Disadvantages

**When you see it:** The prompt describes a trend or development and asks either "What are the advantages and disadvantages?" (neutral — list and explain both sides, no verdict required) or "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" (evaluative — you must decide which side is weightier and commit to that judgement in both your thesis and your conclusion).

**Structure at a glance:** Four paragraphs — an introduction (paraphrase + thesis), one body paragraph developing one side, a second body paragraph developing the other side, and a conclusion. In the **"outweigh"** sub-type the thesis and conclusion must both deliver a clear verdict; in the **neutral** sub-type both remain balanced and verdict-free.

### Fillable sentence frames

#### Introduction — paraphrase
1. It is becoming ever more common for [the group affected] to [restate the action or trend in your own words].
2. The practice of [paraphrase the topic as a gerund phrase] now occupies a prominent place in [the relevant context].
3. *(Higher-risk option — use frames 1 and 2 as your defaults. The bare "In recent years … has become increasingly widespread" pattern sits on examiner memorised-phrase watchlists, so vary the verb and structure heavily if you reach for it):* [Restate the trend as a noun phrase] has expanded rapidly across [the relevant domain] over the past decade.

#### Introduction — thesis / position

*(a) Neutral sub-type — "What are the advantages and disadvantages?" (no verdict):*
1. This essay will examine both the principal benefits and the notable drawbacks of [paraphrase the trend] before reaching a balanced conclusion.
2. While this development offers clear advantages, it also entails certain disadvantages, both of which merit careful consideration.
3. There are compelling arguments on either side of this issue, and the most significant of each will be outlined below.

*(b) "Outweigh" sub-type — "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" (verdict required):*
1. Although [paraphrase the trend] carries some [drawbacks/benefits], I would contend that its [advantages/disadvantages] are ultimately the more significant.
2. This essay will argue that the benefits of [paraphrase the trend] decisively outweigh the associated drawbacks, real though the latter are.
3. In my view, the disadvantages of this development, while genuine, are clearly outweighed by its advantages.

#### Body paragraph 1 — topic sentence
1. One notable [advantage/drawback] of [paraphrase the topic] is that [state the point as a full clause].
2. The most compelling argument [in favour of / against] [the topic] concerns [the point as a noun phrase].
3. To begin with, [the topic] [brings / threatens] [the point as a noun phrase].

#### Body — explanation move ("explain why")
1. This is largely because [provide the underlying reason as a clause].
2. The principal reason for this is that [explain the causal mechanism].
3. This arises since [state the cause], which in turn [state the consequence].

#### Body — example move
1. For instance, [give a concrete, plausible illustration].
2. A clear illustration of this can be seen in [a specific case or scenario].
3. This is well demonstrated by [the example], where [explain what it shows].

#### Body — link-back sentence
1. This consideration therefore represents a significant [benefit/cost] of [the topic].
2. Such an outcome clearly [strengthens / undermines] the case for [the topic].
3. For this reason, [the point] cannot be overlooked when evaluating [the topic].

#### Body paragraph 2 — topic sentence (pivot to the other side)
*Note: Body 2 must signal a contrast with Body 1. In the **neutral** sub-type, present it as an equal counterweight; in the **"outweigh"** sub-type, explicitly signal that this side carries the greater (or lesser) weight, consistent with your thesis.*

*Neutral pivot:*
1. On the other hand, [the topic] also presents a considerable [drawback/advantage], namely [the point].
2. This benefit must, however, be weighed against [the opposing point as a noun phrase].

*"Outweigh" pivot (the side you favour):*
1. Despite this drawback, the more substantial consideration is that [state the stronger point].
2. These concerns notwithstanding, the advantages of [the topic] are far weightier, chiefly because [the decisive point].
3. Nevertheless, such disadvantages remain modest when set against [the decisive advantage].

#### Conclusion

*(a) Neutral sub-type — summarise both sides, no verdict:*
1. In conclusion, [paraphrase the trend] entails both [restate the key advantage] and [restate the key disadvantage], and individuals must weigh these according to their own circumstances.
2. To sum up, while [the benefit] renders this development attractive, [the drawback] ensures that it is not without difficulty.

*(b) "Outweigh" sub-type — restate the verdict:*
1. In conclusion, although [restate the disadvantage] is a legitimate concern, the [advantages restated as a noun phrase] are, on balance, considerably more significant.
2. To conclude, the drawbacks of [the topic] are outweighed by its benefits, and the trend should therefore be regarded as broadly positive.

### Worked example

> An increasing number of employees now work remotely from home instead of commuting to a traditional office. Do the advantages of this development outweigh the disadvantages?

**[Intro — paraphrase + thesis with verdict]** The manner in which people perform their jobs has shifted markedly, with a growing proportion of the workforce now operating from home rather than travelling to a central workplace. Although this arrangement is not without its drawbacks, I would argue that its benefits are considerably more significant.

**[Body 1 — the disadvantage: topic + explanation + example + link-back]** The principal disadvantage of remote working lies in its tendency to erode professional collaboration and social cohesion. This is largely because, when colleagues are physically separated, spontaneous discussions and the informal exchange of ideas become far less frequent, which can slow decision-making and weaken team spirit. A newly recruited graduate, for instance, may struggle to absorb a company's culture or to seek immediate guidance when isolated at home, whereas an office would allow such learning to occur naturally. This loss of interpersonal contact is therefore a genuine cost that organisations must manage with care.

**[Body 2 — the advantages: pivot + explanation + example + link-back]** These concerns notwithstanding, the advantages of this trend are both more numerous and more far-reaching. Most obviously, the elimination of a daily commute returns several hours each week to employees, time that can be devoted to rest, family, or productive work, thereby improving both well-being and output. Furthermore, remote arrangements grant companies access to a global talent pool unconstrained by geography. A technology firm in London, for example, can recruit a skilled engineer based in another country without incurring relocation costs, an option that would have been impractical a decade ago. These gains in flexibility and efficiency clearly carry substantial weight.

**[Conclusion — restate verdict]** In conclusion, while remote working can undeniably weaken collaboration and dilute workplace culture, these difficulties are outweighed by the considerable improvements in flexibility, productivity, and access to talent that it delivers.

### Watch out for
- **Match the framing to the trigger.** A neutral "What are the advantages and disadvantages?" prompt must *not* end in a verdict, while an "outweigh" prompt must *not* sit on the fence — choose your thesis and conclusion frames accordingly.
- **Keep your stance consistent.** If the introduction claims the advantages outweigh the disadvantages, the conclusion must echo the same judgement; examiners penalise a verdict that drifts or reverses.
- **Develop, do not list.** Build each body paragraph around one central point (reason + example + link-back); a second, closely related point is acceptable only when both can be properly developed, as in Body 2 of the model above, where the commute and talent-pool advantages are each extended in full. What scores poorly is a shallow inventory of three or four undeveloped advantages and disadvantages.
- **Do not confuse this with a Discussion essay.** Here you weigh the *merits* of a single trend; you are not presenting and adjudicating two opposing groups' opinions, so avoid "some people argue / others believe" framing.

## 4. Problem/Cause -> Solution

**When you see it:** The task names a trend or situation and asks two linked questions — "What problems does this cause, and what solutions can you suggest?" or "What are the causes of this, and what measures could address them?" You must both diagnose (problem/cause) and prescribe (solution/measure).

**Structure at a glance:** Intro paraphrases the trend and previews one problem/cause + its matched solution; Body 1 states and develops the main problem or cause; Body 2 proposes the solution that directly resolves that exact problem/cause; the conclusion restates the diagnosis and remedy.

> **The matching rule (non-negotiable):** Body 2 must solve the precise problem/cause raised in Body 1 — not a different issue. If Body 1 blames "over-reliance on cars," Body 2 must reduce car use, not (for example) plant more trees. Name the Body 1 problem again inside your Body 2 topic sentence so the examiner sees the link. Tackle the *cause*, not merely the *symptom*.

### Fillable sentence frames

#### Introduction — paraphrase
1. In recent years, [restate the trend in your own words as a noun phrase] has become an increasingly prominent feature of [relevant domain, e.g. modern urban life].
2. It is increasingly observed that [paraphrase the phenomenon as a full clause], an issue that has attracted growing attention.
3. Across many societies, [reword the situation described in the prompt] is now a source of mounting concern.

#### Introduction — thesis / position
1. This essay will identify the main [problem / cause] behind [the trend] and then set out a measure that directly addresses it.
2. The following paragraphs will argue that this situation gives rise to [main problem as a noun phrase] and that [main solution as a noun phrase] offers the most effective response.
3. While the consequences of [the trend] are far from trivial, this essay contends that they can be substantially mitigated through [solution area as a noun phrase].

> *Warning on "tails":* the closing flourish of a frame (the phrase after the comma) is the easiest part for an examiner to recognise as memorised. Reword these tails into your own plainer wording rather than copying them verbatim; a flexible frame survives paraphrase, a fixed phrase does not.

#### Body paragraph 1 — topic sentence (state the cause *or* the problem)
*Cause-led (use when the prompt asks "why" / "what are the causes"):*
1. The principal cause of [the situation] is [reason 1 as a noun phrase].
2. This phenomenon stems largely from [underlying driver as a noun phrase].

*Problem-led (use when the prompt asks "what problems"):*
3. The most serious problem to which [the trend] gives rise is [problem as a noun phrase].
4. A significant difficulty arising from [the trend] is [problem as a noun phrase].

#### Body — explanation move ("explain why")
1. This occurs because [state the mechanism as a full clause].
2. The underlying reason is that [causal explanation as a clause].
3. This is significant because [explain why the consequence matters, as a clause].

#### Body — example move
1. A clear illustration of this can be seen in [specific country / case], where [concrete detail].
2. In [place or context], for instance, [specific evidence or statistic].
3. This is well demonstrated by [specific example], which [the relevant outcome].

#### Body — link-back sentence
1. Consequently, [the cause / problem] undermines [the broader value at stake, e.g. public health].
2. This difficulty therefore poses a genuine threat to [the affected group or system].
3. As a result, [restate the harm in fresh wording], a situation that plainly demands intervention.

#### Body paragraph 2 — topic sentence (propose the *matched* solution)
*Note: unlike Body 1, this paragraph prescribes rather than diagnoses, and it must name the Body 1 problem/cause again so the correspondence is explicit.*
1. The most effective way to address [the exact problem named in Body 1] is to [solution as an infinitive phrase].
2. To counter [the specific cause identified above], [responsible agent, e.g. governments] should [proposed measure].
3. A logical remedy for this [problem / cause] lies in [solution as a noun phrase].

#### Body — "how it helps" move (pairs with the Body 2 topic sentence)
1. This measure would tackle the issue at its root, because [explain the causal link back to Body 1].
2. By [carrying out the solution], [the agent] could [the resulting improvement, phrased to mirror the original problem].
3. Such an approach is effective precisely because it targets [the cause], thereby [the benefit] rather than merely treating the symptoms.

#### Conclusion
1. In conclusion, [the trend] is chiefly responsible for [problem / cause], and [solution] represents the most viable means of resolving it.
2. To conclude, although [the problem] is a formidable challenge, [solution] offers a realistic path towards [desired outcome].
3. In summary, [restate the problem] could be markedly reduced were [the agent] to [restate the solution]. Such a shift would [brief forward-looking outcome].

### Worked example

> In many cities, an increasing number of people are choosing to travel by private car rather than use public transport. What problems does this trend cause, and what solutions can you suggest?

**[Intro — paraphrase + thesis]** In a growing number of urban centres, commuters increasingly favour private vehicles over buses, trains and other forms of public transport. This essay will argue that this preference generates serious congestion and pollution, and that the most effective remedies lie in upgrading public transit and discouraging unnecessary car use.

**[Body 1 — problem + explanation + example + link-back]** The principal difficulty arising from widespread car dependence is the combination of chronic traffic congestion and deteriorating air quality. When the majority of residents drive alone, road networks designed for far lower volumes become saturated, and short journeys can stretch into hours. This matters because idling engines emit large quantities of pollutants, which aggravate respiratory illnesses and burden health systems. In London, for instance, average rush-hour speeds have reportedly fallen below those of the horse-drawn era, while the city repeatedly breaches legal limits on nitrogen dioxide. Such gridlock therefore harms both the economy and public well-being.

**[Body 2 — matched solution + how it helps]** The most logical response is to attack both this congestion and the pollution it generates at their shared source, by making public transport a genuinely attractive alternative while penalising private journeys. Governments could invest heavily in frequent, affordable metro and bus networks, so that commuters no longer regard the car as their only option. This measure directly relieves congestion, because every passenger who switches removes one vehicle from the road. In parallel, a congestion charge of the kind introduced in central London raises the cost of driving and funds further transit improvements, thereby attacking both the pollution and the gridlock identified above.

**[Conclusion — restate diagnosis + remedy]** In conclusion, the surge in private car use is responsible for damaging congestion and pollution, and the surest way to reverse it is to expand public transport and introduce financial disincentives. Were cities to adopt such measures decisively, cleaner air and freer-flowing streets would soon follow.

> **Commentary — why two harms are allowed here:** Body 1 develops *two* problems, congestion and pollution, inside one paragraph. This is admissible only because pollution is a direct by-product of congestion: the two share a single root cause and are therefore cured by a single solution, which is why Body 2 can resolve both at once. Do not read this as licence to list problems. Two *unrelated* problems — say, traffic congestion and a shortage of parking — could not be combined in one body paragraph; each would need its own fully developed problem–solution pair, or you should commit to just one and develop it in depth.

### Watch out for

- **Mismatched solution.** The most common Band-limiting error: Body 2 solves a problem you never raised. Reread Body 1 before drafting Body 2 and reuse its key noun phrase in your solution topic sentence.
- **Confusing "cause" with "problem."** A cause explains *why* the trend exists; a problem is a *harmful consequence* of it. Check which the prompt asks for and answer that one — do not silently switch.
- **Vague, unworkable solutions.** "The government should raise awareness" is too thin. Specify the actor, the mechanism, and the effect, so the examiner can trace how it removes the cause.
- **Listing many problems with no depth.** One fully developed problem plus one matched solution outscores three undeveloped pairs. Resist breadth; reward goes to explanation, example and the logical link. The only exception is two harms that share one root cause, as in the worked example — and even then they must share one solution.

## 5. Two-part / Direct questions

**When you see it:** The prompt makes a statement and then asks you two direct questions, most often "Why is this happening? Is it a positive or negative development?" — but the second question may instead read "Is this a good or a bad thing?", "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?", "Who should be responsible?", or "What can be done?". The defining feature is two distinct questions that must each be answered in full.

**Structure at a glance:** Four paragraphs. The introduction paraphrases the statement and answers BOTH questions in a single combined thesis; Body 1 fully answers Question 1 (e.g. the causes); Body 2 fully answers Question 2 (e.g. your positive/negative judgement); the conclusion restates both answers together.

### Fillable sentence frames

#### Introduction — paraphrase
1. It is becoming increasingly common for [subject of the statement, as a noun phrase] to [restate the described behaviour or trend in your own words].
2. In recent decades, [the described group] have shown a marked tendency to [paraphrase the trend], a shift that shows no sign of slowing.
3. The modern world has witnessed a pronounced rise in [restate the phenomenon as a noun phrase], particularly across [relevant region or demographic].

#### Introduction — thesis (answer BOTH questions at once)
1. This essay will argue that the trend stems chiefly from [cause 1 as a noun phrase] and [cause 2 as a noun phrase], and that, on balance, it constitutes a [positive / negative] development.
2. While this phenomenon can be attributed primarily to [main cause as a noun phrase], I regard its overall impact as predominantly [beneficial / detrimental], for the reasons set out below.
3. The following paragraphs will first explain why [the trend] is occurring, before contending that it should be viewed as a largely [positive / negative] change despite [the opposing consideration, as a NOUN PHRASE].

#### Body paragraph 1 — topic sentence (answers Question 1: "why?")
1. There are two principal reasons that account for [the trend].
2. The emergence of this trend can be explained by a combination of [type of factor, e.g. economic and cultural] factors.
3. Several forces have converged to bring about [restate the phenomenon].

#### Body — explanation move ("explain why")
1. The foremost cause is [reason as a noun phrase], which means that [spell out the logical consequence in a full clause].
2. This situation has arisen largely because [state the cause in a full clause], a circumstance that [explain its effect].
3. A significant driver is [reason as a noun phrase]; as a result, [state the outcome it produces].

#### Body — example move
1. In [relevant country or context], for instance, [give a concrete, plausible illustration of the cause in action].
2. This is clearly illustrated by [specific phenomenon], whereby [briefly describe how it demonstrates the point].
3. A telling example is the way in which [describe a realistic situation that proves the cause].

#### Body — link-back sentence
1. For these reasons, it is unsurprising that [the trend] has become so widespread.
2. Such conditions make [restate the behaviour] an almost inevitable response.
3. Taken together, these factors go a long way towards explaining [the phenomenon].

#### Body paragraph 2 — topic sentence (answers Question 2: the judgement)
*Note: unlike Body 1, which reports causes neutrally, this paragraph must take an explicit evaluative stance and defend it, usually conceding the opposing side before reaffirming your position. State the verdict directly as positive or negative — do not borrow "outweigh", which belongs to the Advantages/Disadvantages and "do the advantages outweigh" types.*
*Do not lift any opener verbatim — vary at least the first six words. A long, distinctive phrase reproduced word-for-word is exactly what an examiner recognises as bolt-on memorised language and discounts.*
1. Weighing these effects, I consider the trend, on the whole, a [beneficial / harmful] one.
2. Turning to the question of its desirability, I believe its consequences are, in the final analysis, decidedly [positive / negative].
3. In my view, this trend should be regarded as a fundamentally [positive / negative] one, notwithstanding the reservations it may provoke.

#### Conclusion — restate BOTH answers
1. In conclusion, [the trend] arises chiefly from [cause(s)], and although [the opposing consideration] merits attention, it remains, in my judgement, a [positive / negative] development.
2. To summarise, the principal driver of this change is [main cause], and despite [the concession, as a NOUN PHRASE or -ing clause, e.g. "the risk of isolation" / "there being some risks"], its overall effect is decidedly [beneficial / harmful].
3. Overall, while the causes lie in [cause(s)] and the risks of [drawback] are real, the trend is one that ought to be [welcomed / resisted — pick ONE to match your verdict] on balance.

*After* despite *or* in spite of *use a noun phrase or -ing clause, never a finite clause (write "despite the risk of isolation", not "despite some people lose contact"). Wherever a frame shows a slash, choose ONE option to fit your verdict — never leave both in the final essay.*

### Worked example

> In many countries today, a growing number of people choose to live alone rather than with family or housemates. Why is this happening? Is this a positive or a negative development?
>
> Write at least 250 words.

**[Intro — paraphrase + dual thesis]**
It is becoming ever more common for individuals across the developed world to occupy their own homes rather than sharing accommodation with relatives or friends. This trend is driven primarily by rising affluence and shifting social values, and although it carries certain drawbacks, I consider it, overall, a positive development.

**[Body 1 — answers "why is this happening?"]**
The principal reason behind this shift is the growth in personal income. As wages have risen across most industrialised economies, a far greater proportion of the population can now afford the rent or mortgage payments that solitary living entails — a lifestyle that would have been financially out of reach for earlier generations. A second contributing factor is the gradual transformation of cultural attitudes. Whereas remaining in the parental home until marriage was once the norm, contemporary society increasingly prizes independence and self-reliance, so young adults tend to move out as soon as they are able. The expansion of higher education, which draws students away from their home towns, has reinforced this tendency.

**[Body 2 — answers "positive or negative?"]**
On balance, I regard this shift as a positive one. Living alone allows people to cultivate practical competencies, such as budgeting and household management, while affording them the privacy to pursue their own interests without compromise. Equally, solitary living frees people to structure their daily routines entirely around their own commitments — choosing when to work, eat and socialise — which can markedly improve well-being and productivity. Admittedly, critics warn that solitary residence may foster loneliness, particularly among the elderly. Nevertheless, modern communication technology enables individuals to sustain close relationships regardless of physical distance, which substantially mitigates this risk.

**[Conclusion — restate both answers]**
In conclusion, the rise in solo living stems chiefly from greater prosperity and an evolving emphasis on independence. While the threat of isolation should not be dismissed, the personal growth and freedom that this lifestyle offers render it, in my judgement, a largely beneficial trend.

### Watch out for
- **Answering only one question.** This is the single biggest band-killer for this type. If you explain the causes brilliantly but forget to state whether the trend is positive or negative (or vice versa), you cannot exceed Band 5 for Task Achievement no matter how good the English is.
- **Letting your "position" drift between the intro and Body 2.** Your combined thesis must commit to a positive *or* negative verdict, and Body 2 plus the conclusion must defend that same verdict — do not hedge with "it has both good and bad sides" and leave it there.
- **Confusing this with the Advantages/Disadvantages type.** Here Question 1 is usually a *cause* question ("why?"), not a benefit. Resist the urge to list advantages in Body 1; reserve all evaluation for Body 2. State your Body 2 verdict directly as positive or negative — save "outweigh" for the dedicated outweigh type.
- **Unbalanced paragraphs.** Each question deserves a full, roughly equal body paragraph with its own topic sentence, explanation and example. A two-line answer to Question 2 tacked onto Question 1 signals an underdeveloped response.

## After you fill in a frame — the 6-point self-check

Run this over every essay before you stop:

1. **Type & obligation** — did I use the right structure, and did I honour its non-negotiable (verdict? opinion in both places? matched solution? both questions answered?)?
2. **No brackets left** — every `[placeholder]` replaced with my own words; no slash-options (`positive / negative`) left unchosen.
3. **Stance is consistent** — intro, body, and conclusion all point the same way; no mid-essay drift.
4. **Variants rotated** — I didn't reuse the exact same opener/linker phrasing as my last essay; tails reworded.
5. **PEEL in each body** — Point, **Explain the mechanism**, specific Example, Link back. (The *Explain* step is what Band 6 essays skip.)
6. **~5–10 connectives total, varied** — plus referencing (this trend / such measures / the former). A linker on every sentence is a Band 6 tell — see the bank in [task2-essay-templates.md](task2-essay-templates.md#part-2--the-connective-word-bank-exhaustive).

> These frames were generated and then revised against a strict-examiner critique pass (memorized-template risk, connective-grammar, register, and Task Response were each checked). The worked essays were assessed at **Band 8–8.5**. Treat them as a ceiling to aim for, not a script to copy.
