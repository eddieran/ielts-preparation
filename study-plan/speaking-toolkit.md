# Speaking Toolkit (Parts 1–3) → Band 7+

11–14 min, with a real examiner (in person or video), recorded. Four criteria, 25% each: **Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, Pronunciation.** Your local app has **no** speaking module, so this runs on recording + AI tools (SmallTalk2Me, ELSA). Self-mark against the Speaking descriptor table in the [dossier](./IELTS-2026-reference-dossier.md).

> **The single biggest lever:** never give one-line answers, and make your pauses about *ideas*, not *searching for words*. That one shift is most of Fluency 6 → 7.

---

## Part 1 — Interview (4–5 min, ~12 short questions on familiar topics)

**Framework: ARE — Answer → Reason → Example.** Aim for 2–4 sentences. Direct one-word answers cap fluency.

> *"Do you like cooking?" → "Yes, I genuinely enjoy it. I find it quite therapeutic after a long day at work, and recently I've been experimenting with Thai food — my green curry last weekend turned out surprisingly well."* (Answer / Reason / Example)

Topics rotate around home, hometown, work/study, plus 1–2 familiar themes (food, weather, hobbies, technology, travel).

---

## Part 2 — Long turn (cue card; 1 min prep → speak 1–2 min)

**In the 1-minute prep:** jot **keyword notes only** (4–6 words), plus 1–2 strong vocab items. **Never write full sentences** and never read notes aloud.

**Delivery:**
- Open by addressing the topic directly.
- Use the bullets as a skeleton but **expand** each with a story, comparison, or feeling (~30–40 s per bullet).
- Frame with **Past → Present → Future** to guarantee you never run dry.
- Save the last bullet ("…and explain why") as your closer.
- **Keep talking until the examiner stops you.** Stopping at ~50 s caps fluency.

**Recent cue-card topics (2025–2026) to rehearse:** a person who inspires you · a useful technology/app (or one hard to use) · a time a device broke down · an important email/message · a skill you've learned or want to learn · a memorable/disrupted journey · advice you gave or received · a community/environmental event · a problem you solved · a book/film that impressed you · a place you'd like to visit · a creative person you know.

---

## Part 3 — Discussion (4–5 min, abstract — **where the band is decided**)

Treat it as a mini-essay spoken aloud. **Use PEEL** (Point → Explain → Example → Link) and shift from "I" to **general/societal** language ("people tend to…", "in many societies…").

**Hedge and speculate** — this is the clearest Band 7–8 signal:
- "It largely depends on…" · "To some extent…" · "It's highly likely that…"
- "Were the government to invest more, …" (inversion = complex grammar points)
- Counter then answer: "Some would argue X, but personally I think Y."

**Buy thinking time without dead air:**
- "That's a thought-provoking question — off the top of my head…"
- "I'd never really considered that, but I suppose…"

> Do **not** treat Part 3 like Part 1 (short personal answers). Go abstract, balanced, and developed.

---

## Phrase banks

**Replace fillers** ("um, uh, like, you know") with silent micro-pauses or the thinking phrases above.

**Linking (vary these):** Furthermore · On top of that · That said · Having said that · As a result · Which means that…

**Opinion:** Personally, I'd say… · I'm a firm believer that… · If you ask me…

**Natural collocations / idioms (deploy 1–2 per answer — don't overload):**
a steep learning curve · strike a balance · a double-edged sword · broaden your horizons · a blessing in disguise · few and far between · the bigger picture · take its toll · second to none · in the long run.

---

## Pronunciation (it's 25% — and easy points)

- Focus on **suprasegmentals**: word stress, sentence stress, intonation, and **chunking** (group words, pause at meaning units).
- **Do NOT slow down and over-enunciate** — it destroys rhythm and *lowers* the score.
- **Your accent is irrelevant.** Only intelligibility matters. Band 7 = all Band-6 features **plus some** Band-8 features.
- Tool: **ELSA Speak** for any word you mispronounce; **shadow** native audio (YouTube/Cambridge Speaking videos) daily — repeat right after the speaker to absorb rhythm.

---

## Band 6 → 7 checklist

- [ ] **Speaks at length** with only *language-related* hesitation (pauses to think of an idea, not a word)
- [ ] No one-line answers anywhere; Part 2 runs the full ~2 min
- [ ] Range of connectives used **flexibly** (not "and… so… because" on repeat)
- [ ] Some **less-common / idiomatic** vocabulary, used appropriately
- [ ] **Paraphrases** smoothly when a word won't come (describe it — that's a *skill* signal)
- [ ] **Frequent error-free** complex sentences (mix complex with clean simple)
- [ ] Clear word/sentence stress + intonation; easy to understand throughout
- [ ] Part 3 answers are abstract, balanced, hedged — not short and personal

---

## The practice loop

1. **Record** an answer (phone or SmallTalk2Me) to a real question.
2. Listen back **twice**: once for **fluency** (count fillers + long pauses), once for **range** (did you use complex structures + collocations?).
3. **Re-record the same answer** applying ONE fix.
4. Weekly: do a full P1–P2–P3 sim on SmallTalk2Me and compare its band + your self-mark to last week's.

> Daily minimum on a collapsed day: record **one** Part 2 cue card and re-record it once. Speaking improves only by speaking.
