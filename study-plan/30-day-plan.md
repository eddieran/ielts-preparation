## 1. The strategy (read this once, then trust it)

**The band math decides where your time goes.** Overall 7.5 = the four skills averaging ≥7.25 (which rounds up). A half-band is *cheap* to win in Listening/Reading (auto-marked, pure technique) and *expensive* in Writing. So:

| Skill | Your likely now | **Target** | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Listening | ~7.0 | **8.0** | Most trainable in a month; your score engine |
| Reading | ~7.0 | **8.0** (min 7.5) | Technique + pacing; second engine |
| Writing | ~6.0–6.5 | **7.0** | Hardest band to move; 7.0 is enough if L/R hit 8 |
| Speaking | ~6.5 | **7.0–7.5** | Daily reps move this fast |

Any of these combinations lands 7.5 overall: `8/8/7/7`, `8/7.5/7/7`, `8/8/6.5/7`, `7.5/7.5/7/7`. **Writing does not have to reach 7.5.** That removes the pressure from your hardest skill and makes the target realistic in a month.

**Three rules that govern every day:**
1. **Touch a productive skill (Writing OR Speaking) every single day** — these only improve by *producing* and *getting feedback*, not by reading tips.
2. **Every Writing/Speaking rep ends in a feedback loop:** produce → get AI feedback (or self-mark vs the official descriptor) → **redo/rewrite the weak part**. The rewrite is where the band moves, not the first attempt.
3. **Practise Listening & Reading on screen** (you're computer-delivered) and always *timed*. Use the British Council "IELTS on Computer" practice and ieltsonlinetests.com.

**Honest expectation:** 7.5 overall in 4 weeks at 1–2 hrs/day is *achievable* on this plan because L/R carry the average. The risk is Writing — if it stalls at 6.5, you can still make 7.5 (see `8/8/6.5/7`), and **One Skill Retake** (computer-only, within 60 days, one skill, keep the other three) is your safety net for whichever single skill narrowly misses. Diagnose early; keep OSR in reserve.

## 3. Daily structure (two budgets — use whichever the day allows)

**TIGHT day (~60 min):** 10-min warm-up · 40-min one productive block (W or S) · 10-min receptive micro-drill.

**FULL day (~90–120 min):** 10-min warm-up · 50–60-min productive block **with feedback + rewrite** · 25–30-min timed L or R section · 5-min log in tracker.

Protect the **productive block** above all else. If a day collapses to 20 minutes, do one Speaking recording or rewrite three sentences — never zero.

## 6. The feedback loop (the engine of the whole plan)

**Writing:** write timed → paste into LexiBot/Speechful → read the per-criterion notes → **rewrite only the flagged sentences/paragraph** → finally compare against a Cambridge model answer for the same question type. Keep a running "my top 5 grammar errors" list and hunt them in every draft.

**Speaking:** record (phone or SmallTalk2Me) → listen back once for **fluency** (count fillers/pauses) and once for **range** (did you use complex structures + collocations?) → **re-record the same answer** applying one fix. Use ELSA for any word you mispronounce.

**Listening/Reading:** never just check the score. For each miss, label the *cause* (synonym not recognised / spelling / plural / Not-Given inference / ran out of time) and drill that cause. Your error log is more valuable than your score.

---

## 7. If you fall behind

- **Lost a day?** Don't double up and burn out. Just keep the chain: do the 20-min minimum (one Speaking recording or one paragraph rewrite).
- **Time is genuinely 60 min/day every day?** Drop the secondary L/R block on Writing/Speaking days and rely on the two weekend mocks for L/R volume — but never drop the productive block or its rewrite.
- **Writing won't move past 6.5 by Week 4?** Accept it (the `8/8/6.5/7` path still gives 7.5), maximise L/R/S, and plan a **One Skill Retake** for Writing if the official result lands at 7.0 overall. Book the full test early enough that the 60-day OSR window covers any deadline.
